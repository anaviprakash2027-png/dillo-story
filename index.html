Dillo Day: The Who, What, and Why of Northwestern’s Biggest Day of The Year

Come May 16, about 10,000 attendees will swarm Northwestern University’s Lakefill for the most anticipated event of the academic year: Dillo Day.

The 54th iteration of the nation’s largest student-run music festival is themed “Dillo Speedway” and brings “bold colors,” “electric energy” and “sleek outfits,” according to its theme announcement post.

Mayfest Productions, the student organization behind Dillo Day, also announced the creation of a third stage as part of its lineup. The Burrow aims to diversify voices and genres heard at Dillo Day, and is co-organized by the Korean American Student Association, Chinese Students Association, Middle Eastern North African Student Association and WNUR.

Along with Dillo Day’s Main Stage, the festival’s Second Stage has been organized by For Members Only since 2019 and has had an all-Black artist line-up every year. Prior to FMO, WNUR organized the stage’s line-up.

Performers range in genre, hometown

Dillo Day has seen over 100 artists in the last 25 years, performing everything from Rock to K-Pop. While deliberating which artists to feature in each year’s festival, Mayfest puts out artist polls for Northwestern students to fill out.

The latest poll for the 54th Dillo Day asks respondents to rate the following genres in terms of what they want to hear at the event: Afrobeats, Country, EDM, Hip-Hop/Rap, Indie/Alternative, K-Pop, Latin/Reggaeton, Pop, Rock/Metal and R&B/Soul.

Some Dillo Day headliners have seen major success and gone on to win major awards later in their career. Kendrick Lamar, who performed at the festival in 2012, has the most Grammys of any rapper, winning his first in 2015 and latest in 2026.

Mayfest’s artist poll also asks students to rate their interests in certain artists, from Audrey Hobert, who is from New York City, to Inhaler, a band based in Dublin, Ireland.

Since 2016, Mayfest has also hosted competitions for student performers leading up to Dillo Day. Both the winners of Battle of the Bands and Battle of the DJs get to showcase their art on the music festival’s Main Stage on the big day. The 2025 winners were Interia and Perlmutter, respectively.

Production work engages 10 committees, around 100 members

Mayfest oversees 10 committees that focus on every aspect of putting the music festival together. From booking acts to building the Dillo Day app, everything is student-run. Applications to join come out in September to allow the group almost an entire school year to prepare for the music festival. Roughly 100 students partake in the initiative.

However, the organization — and its mainstage event — used to be this big.

The first Dillo Day took place in 1973 as a low-budget music festival hosted by a group of friends, according to Northwestern Magazine.

Mayfest was founded a few years later in 1977. It began as the organizer of various Spring Quarter events, including A&O Productions’ Spring Festival, Greek Week and Dillo Day. Soon after, the latter took over as Mayfest Productions’ sole operation.

In just the last seven years, the festival has expanded to include over $297,000 in annual ticket sales — even with NU undergraduates getting in for free — and over 9,000 people in attendance each year following two virtual festivals during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Today, Dillo Day goes beyond the music, including sponsorships from organizations like the Chicago White Sox and Smartwater. It also engages the Northwestern community leading up to the event with the artist polls as well as a week of spirit events preceding the music festival. It also announces its annual theme in partnership with Northwestern’s dining halls.

While Dillo Speedway is still a few months away, performers and related programming information are expected to be announced throughout the spring, leading up to the big day on May 16.
